Learn About GeoBase

What is the GeoBase Initiative?

The GeoBase initiative aims to provide access to consistent, national scale, current and maintained, geospatial foundational data for all of Canada. Foundational geospatial data provides location reference, context and underpinning for a wide variety of scientific, socio-economic, business and other data. The use of foundational layers allows an environment – business, social, ecological, etc. – to be analyzed by geographic location which often reveals information that results in more effective policy and decision making, by and for governments, private sector companies and individual citizens.

The foundation of GeoBase is collaboration. To date, government organizations representing all of the provinces, territories and several federal government departments have, through; formal partnership agreements, joint project funding for data acquisition, sharing of source data, in kind data processing, and by cooperating on defining data models and setting national standards, participated in realizing Geobase. By working together, these government organizations have increased their efficiency in collecting and maintaining foundational geospatial layers by reducing duplication of effort. Next step for GeoBase is to consider that participating agencies have more to offer. Data is and will keep on being an important deliverable of the GeoBase initiative. Nevertheless past years have proven that such datasets have been achieved through the use of systems (broad sense) and expertise. These three are key ingredients to be used for nowadays needs and requirements for geospatial data, services and applications. The future will see the circle of collaboration grow to include contributions from other organizations, both governmental and non-governmental.

The foundational geospatial layers making up the GeoBase collection is distributed in accordance with national standards, including explicit metadata to facilitate its use. GeoBase is the key national foundational layer component of the Canadian Geospatial Data Infrastructure (CGDI) and of the federal geospatial platform.

Through GEO.ca and Open Maps, users can access these quality foundational geospatial layers without cost, without restrictions, under the Government of Canada Open Government Licence – Canada.

What is the GeoBase Initiative's vision?

High quality foundational geospatial layers for Canada: collected once, used many times; maintained and/or updated regularly; available without restrictions under an open data license.

What is the GeoBase Initiative's mission?

The GeoBase initiative aims to provide access to consistent, national scale, current and maintained geospatial foundational data for all of Canada.

How is the GeoBase Initiative organized?

The GeoBase initiative began when multiple government mapping agencies realised that current resource levels allocated to their tasks were inadequate.

At its annual meeting in Fredrickton, October 2001, the Canadian Council on Geomatics (CCOG), a partnership of federal, provincial and territorial government mapping organizations, approved GeoBase’s vision, principles and data definitions.

The Geobase Steering Committee

CCOG also created the GeoBase Steering Committee and commissioned it to develop and propose and action plan.

This initiative was to be the beginning of a new era of cooperation between various federal, provincial and territorial data-producing stakeholders that is still ongoing today.

Built on the principle of “collect once – use many times”, it was to become the source of fundamental geospatial framework data for Canada providing authoritative data, national in scope for many Canadian geospatial applications.

Launch

GeoBase was launched in November 2003 with free and open access to six layers of geospatial framework data without cost, without restrictions and under a common data license

In the decades since, millions of datasets have been downloaded, new data layers have been added, and national data standards have been defined collaboratively and adopted by the CCOG.

Natural Resources Canada (NRCan), the CCOG and the GeoBase Steering Committee continuously seek to improve the quality of the data and access to it, and are keenly aware of the need to evolve the data offerings to meet the changing needs of Canadian Geospatial data users.

Who is involved in the GeoBase Initiative?

Canadian Council on Geomatics

The Canadian Council of Geomatics (CCOG), created in 1972, is the major federal-provincial-territorial consultative body for geographic information management.

CCOG meets twice a year, to provide a forum for exchanging information on programs, to consider common operational issues, to discuss proposed legislation relevant to geomatics (particularly land surveying), and to develop and promote national geomatics standards.

CCOG has endorsed the vision and principles behind GeoBase, supports the GeoBase action plan, and commissioned a Steering Committee to carry out its objectives.

Geobase Steering Committee

The GeoBase Steering Committee is made up of provincial, territorial and federal representatives headed by a Federal Chair. The Steering Committee develops the strategy for the implamentation of GeoBase and provides ongoing support and direction.

The Steering Committee also promotes GeoBase to:

  • Canadian stakeholder
  • Federal, provincial and territorial ministers
  • Various Geomatics-related committees and associations
  • The Minister’s National Advisory Board on Earth Sciences of Natural Resources
  • The Federal Inter-Agency Committee on Geomatics (IACG)
  • The Geomatics Industry Association of Canada

Geobase Secretariat

The GeoBase Secretariat, hosted by NRCan, works to support the GeoBase Steering Committee (SC) in their efforts to implement GeoBase. It is funded by contributions from the provinces, territories, federal departments and GeoConnections. The Secretariat will:

  • Monitor, coordinates project plans and report to the SC on project progress
  • Assemble financial information regarding the implementation of GeoBase and report to the SC on such
  • Recommend and write the Terms of Reference for Working Groups and Advisory for review and approval by the SC.
  • Provide support to GeoBase related activities and committees
What type of principles does the GeoBase initiative uphold?

The following principles provide underpinning for the decision making processes guiding the operation of GeoBase. The CCOG member organizations act in accordance with these principles in their collaboration on GeoBase. As other organizations join in supporting GeoBase – as data providers/custodian, as user stakeholders, or as observers – they will be expected to respect these principles.

1) GeoBase data is available at no cost.

2) GeoBase has no restrictions on its subsequent use.

GeoBase data users may use and re-distribute the data either as is or as part of a value added product or service. The only restriction is that the user may not alter the data and represent those alterations as GeoBase data.

3) GeoBase data is available under an open government data license.

GeoBase data is available using the Government of Canada Open Data License. The license is available at: http://data.gc.ca/eng/open-government-license-canada.

4) GeoBase data provides national data coverage.

Inclusion of data on GeoBase will be dependent on the potential for the data to be available as a national dataset. Partial datasets may be included as works in progress. Certain datasets, for example orthoimagery, may contain multiple resolutions dependent on data availability and use (for example, high resolution data is more commonly required in highly populated regions).

5) GeoBase data is collected once and used by many.

A key tenet of the GeoBase continues to be the elimination or at least, significant reduction of duplication of effort. Collaboration on data acquisition may be coordinated and/or negotiated by any data provider/custodian.

6) GeoBase data is collected, maintained and/or validated by trusted sources

Service providers both within various levels of government and outside of government rely on GeoBase data. When committing to contributing data to GeoBase, the data provider/custodian also commits to maintaining and/or validating the data and to providing continual availability of that data for GeoBase users.

This principle recognizes that while only one party will be maintaining the data, other parties benefit from the maintenance. Therefore, cost of maintenance may be shared through agreements as required and negotiated. It is expected that the maintenance is carried out in as economical a manner as possible, using methods determined to be most efficient and effective by the data provider/custodian.

7) GeoBase fulfills requests, requirements and provides solutions to issues by first using existing authoritative data. Transformation, integration and management of existing authoritative data are the essence of this principle.

8) As expressed previously, integration is one of the concepts that GeoBase prioritizes. The alternate solution is for participating organizations to collectively acquire data that is missing.

9) GeoBase deliverables and efforts are publically available, all GeoBase efforts are broadly communicated. In doing so, GeoBase contributes to a better knowledge of geomatics and related domains.

10) GeoBase expands principle number five to build once, use many! and applies it to data, systems and expertise.

11) GeoBase promotes standards and supports the Canadian Geospatial Data Infrastructure (CGDI). GeoBase focuses on interoperability of existing infrastructure (eg. provincial SDIs, FGP).

12) GeoBase will be concentrated primarily on governments issues (all levels) thus encompassing the need for new or updated data, systems or expertise.

What are some of the technical characteristics of the data?

1) GeoBase data are standardized and the standards are accessible.

GeoBase data standards have been developed and adopted as national standards by the Canadian Council on Geomatics. These standards include the data model (or models in some cases) as well as collection and maintenance standards that define accuracy and currency requirements of the data. Data standards for each of the GeoBase data layers are described in product specifications published on the ___________ website.

In addition, in order to maximize the interoperability of GeoBase data, international standards are – as much as possible – adhered to when developing GeoBase data standard and GeoBase tools and services.

2) Geobase metadata are defined and standardized at the entity level.

All GeoBase data layers include ISO 19115 (North American Data Profile) compliant metadata.

3) GeoBase data are seamless across Canada.

GeoBase data is edge matched to ensure seamlessness across jurisdictional boundaries.

4) GeoBase data are consistent.

As far as technically possible and feasible, geobase data layers are vertically aligned.

5) GeoBase data elements carry a unique identifier.

Each data element has a unique identifier (UID) and every instance and representation of that element carries the same UID. This enables different geometric representation of data to share attributes. For the GeoBase data collection, UIDs are described as Universal Unique Identifiers (UUIDs) as defined and documented in the Product Specifications for each of the GeoBase data layers.

High Resolution Digital Elevation Model

The High Resolution Digital Elevation Model (HRDEM) product is derived from airborne LiDAR data (mainly in the south) and satellite images in the north. The complete coverage of the Canadian territory is gradually being established. It includes a Digital Terrain Model (DTM), a Digital Surface Model (DSM) and other derived data. For DTM datasets, derived data available are slope, aspect, shaded relief, color relief and color shaded relief maps and for DSM datasets, derived data available are shaded relief, color relief and color shaded relief maps.
